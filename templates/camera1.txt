                                    <p style="text-align: justify; text-justify: inter-word;">Corn leaf blight is a fungal disease that affects corn plants, typically caused by pathogens such as the fungi 
                                        Exserohilum turcicum and Bipolaris maydis. This disease manifests as irregularly shaped lesions or spots on the 
                                        leaves of the corn plant, which may start as small, water-soaked areas and eventually enlarge to form large, tan to 
                                        brown lesions with dark borders. In severe cases, the lesions can merge, leading to extensive damage to the leaves. 
                                        Corn leaf blight can reduce photosynthetic efficiency, weaken the plant, and result in yield losses if left unmanaged. 
                                        Warm and humid weather conditions, along with high plant densities and susceptible corn varieties, create favorable 
                                        environments for the development and spread of corn leaf blight.</p>
                                    <ul class="list-inline" style="text-align: justify; text-justify: inter-word;">
                                        <li>
                                            <strong style="text-align: center;">Symtoms:</strong>
                                            <li>One of the primary symptoms is the appearance of irregularly shaped lesions or spots on the leaves. 
                                            These lesions often start as small, water-soaked areas, which gradually enlarge over time. As the disease progresses, 
                                            the lesions develop into larger, tan to brown patches with dark margins. In severe cases, these lesions can coalesce, 
                                            leading to extensive blighting of the leaf tissue. Additionally, infected leaves may exhibit signs of chlorosis or yellowing
                                            surrounding the lesions, further compromising the plant’s ability to photosynthesize. In some instances, the disease may also 
                                            affect other parts of the plant, such as the stalks and husks, causing similar lesions and discoloration.</li>
                                        </li>
                                        <br>
                                        <li>
                                            <strong>Cure:</strong>
                                            <li style="list-style-type: circle; list-style-position: inside;">
                                                Planting corn varieties that are resistant to the pathogens causing leaf blight, as this can significantly 
                                                reduce the susceptibility of the plants to infection.</li>
                                            <li style="list-style-type: circle; list-style-position: inside;">
                                                Implementing crop rotation practices can help break the disease cycle by reducing the buildup of pathogens in the soil. </li>
                                            <li style="list-style-type: circle; list-style-position: inside;">
                                                Maintaining proper spacing between plants and adequate air circulation can also help create unfavorable 
                                                conditions for the development and spread of leaf blight.</li>
                                            <li style="list-style-type: circle; list-style-position: inside;">
                                                Application of biocontrol agents or beneficial microorganisms in the soil to suppress the growth of pathogens.</li>
                                            <li style="list-style-type: circle; list-style-position: inside;">
                                                Adequate nutrient management, including balanced fertilization and soil amendment practices, can also promote 
                                                plant health and enhance resistance to diseases like corn leaf blight.</li>
                                            <li style="list-style-type: circle; list-style-position: inside;">
                                                Implementing timely irrigation practices that avoid prolonged leaf wetness periods can help minimize the risk of fungal infection and spread.</li>
                                            <li style="list-style-type: circle; list-style-position: inside;">
                                                Practicing good sanitation measures, such as removing and destroying infected plant debris, can help reduce inoculum levels

                                    <h2 class="text-uppercase">Leaf Spot</h2>
                                    <p class="item-intro text-muted">This corn leaf picture has a leaf spot disease</p>
                                    <img class="img-fluid d-block mx-auto" src="../static/assets/img/spot.jpg" alt="leaf spot" />
                                    <p style="text-align: justify; text-justify: inter-word;">
                                        Corn leaf spot disease, also known as northern or southern corn leaf blight depending on the specific pathogens 
                                        involved, is a fungal infection that affects corn plants worldwide. The disease is primarily caused by fungi of 
                                        the genus Bipolaris, including Bipolaris maydis and Bipolaris zeicola, as well as the fungus Exserohilum turcicum. 
                                        Corn leaf spot manifests as circular to oval-shaped lesions or spots on the leaves, initially appearing as small, 
                                        water-soaked areas that later develop into brown to tan spots with dark margins. Corn leaf spot can spread rapidly 
                                        under favorable environmental conditions, including warm temperatures, high humidity, and prolonged leaf wetness periods. </p>
                                    <ul class="list-inline" style="text-align: justify; text-justify: inter-word;">
                                        <li>
                                            <strong style="text-align: center;">Symtoms:</strong>
                                            <li>One of the primary symptoms of corn leaf spot is the appearance of small, circular to oval-shaped lesions 
                                                or spots on the leaves. These lesions typically start as tiny, water-soaked areas that eventually develop 
                                                into brown to tan spots with dark margins. As the disease progresses, the spots may enlarge and coalesce, 
                                                leading to the formation of irregularly shaped lesions. In severe cases, the lesions can cover a significant 
                                                portion of the leaf surface, resulting in extensive blighting and necrosis of the affected tissue. Infected 
                                                leaves may also exhibit signs of chlorosis or yellowing around the lesions, which further compromises the plant’s 
                                                ability to photosynthesize and can lead to reduced yield and quality. Also, corn leaf spot may affect other parts 
                                                of the plant, including the stalks and husks, causing similar lesions and discoloration.</li>
                                        </li>
                                        <br>
                                        <li>
